## Project center

- Participated in the refurbishment and recommissioning of a legacy acrylic vacuum chamber used for electric-propulsion testing.
- Focused on pump-down verification, pressure-gauge validation, internal actuator structure installation, and ion-probe motor integration.
- This was primarily a lab-infrastructure reliability effort rather than a full research study.

## Context anchor

Electric propulsion and plasma diagnostics require:

- Stable low-pressure operation.
- Correct pump sequencing (roughing to turbo).
- Functional, trustworthy pressure instrumentation.
- Rigid internal mounting for probes and actuators.

Because the chamber is acrylic, controlled pump-down and seal integrity are especially important to avoid overstress or leaks. The work here concentrated on restoring operational discipline and hardware readiness so that future tests start from a known-good vacuum system.

## Vacuum system overview

- Representative acrylic vacuum chamber backed by a mechanical roughing pump and turbomolecular pump.
- KF40 hardware used for modular vacuum plumbing, instrumentation ports, and optical or mechanical feedthroughs.
- Internal actuator structure provides probe translation for in-vacuum diagnostics.

## Workflow map

### Mechanical and structural work

- Assembled the internal linear-actuator structure for probe translation.
- Prepared and installed the stepper motor mount, with motor location and fit-up verified against chamber clearances.
- Reattached optical and mechanical connections to the chamber where needed.

### Vacuum plumbing

- Connected the roughing pump to the chamber manifold.
- Inspected KF40 flanges, clamps, and seals for damage or missing components.
- Identified missing KF40 connectors and initiated the quote and ordering process.
- Re-established the rule that the turbomolecular pump only operates when backed by an active roughing pump.

### Pump-down verification

- Operated the roughing pump and monitored the pressure transducer response over an extended interval.
- Observed limited apparent pressure change, flagging possible pressure-transducer or configuration issues.
- Established a requirement to run the roughing pump for a defined duration before assessing chamber performance.

### Procurement and tracking

- Investigated the status of the stepper motor hardware and any prior orders.
- Coordinated Quartzy entries and supplier follow-up for missing KF40 and motor components.
- Ensured new parts are tracked rather than pulled informally from other test stands.

## Why this matters

Vacuum systems often fail due to:

- Improper pump sequencing.
- Assumptions about equipment condition.
- Faulty or unverified instrumentation.
- Small but critical missing hardware pieces.

This project reinforced a vacuum verification mindset:

- Do not assume turbopump operation - verify backing conditions first.
- Do not judge pump-down performance prematurely - allow sufficient runtime and check diagnostics.
- Do not run the turbo without an active backing pump.

The outcome is a chamber that can safely support electric-propulsion testing once final motor integration and instrumentation checks are complete.

## Results at a glance

- **Goal:** restore a partially idle acrylic vacuum chamber to test-ready condition for electric-propulsion diagnostics.
- **How we check it:** roughing pump validation, pressure response checks, hardware integrity review, and confirmed turbo backing protocol.
- **Current focus:** integrating the stepper motor and verifying the full roughing to turbo pump sequence.
- **What we measure:** pressure decay curves, achievable base pressure, transducer response, and structural alignment of the probe actuator.
